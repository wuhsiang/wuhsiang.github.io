An example of Independence Metropolis Algorithm (with R code)

2015-10-20
Data Scientist
Comments

Algorithm

We can present the independence Metropolis algorithm as follows[^rossi]:

  1. Start with $$ \theta_{0} $$.
  2. Draw $$ \vartheta \sim q_{\text{imp}} $$.
  3. Compute $$ \alpha = \text{min} {1, \frac{\pi(\vartheta) / q{\text{imp}}(\vartheta)}{\pi(\theta) / q{\text{imp}}(\theta)} } $$.
  4. With probability $$ \alpha $$, $$ \theta{1} = \vartheta $$, else $$ \theta{1} = \theta_{0} $$.
  5. Repeat, as necessary.

If $$ q $$ is very close to $$ \pi $$, then most draws will be accepted since the ratio in the $$ \alpha $$ computation will be close to one. The independence Metropolis chain always works well for low-dimensional problems.

R Implementation

We consider a simple example:
$$
y = \alpha + \beta x + \epsilon, \epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^{2}),
$$
and test the Independence Metropolis algorithm by simulated data. We set $$ \alpha = -2 $$, $$ \beta = 4 $$ and $$ \sigma = 1 $$.

An example of Gibbs sampler (with R code)

2015-10-20
Data Scientist
Comments

Algorithm

Gibbs sampler cycles through a set of conditional distributions of $$\pi$$. If we break $$\theta$$ into several “groups” or “blocks” of parameters, then the Gibbs sampler can be presented as[^rossi]:

  1. Start with initial values $$\theta_{0}$$.
  2. Sample from
    $$\begin{array}
    {lll}
    \theta{11} & \sim & f{1}(\theta{1} | \theta{02}, …,\theta{0p}) \
    \theta    {12} & \sim & f{2}(\theta{2} | \theta{11}, \theta{03}, …,\theta{0p}) \
    .. & .. & .. \
    \theta    {1p} & \sim & f{p}(\theta{p} | \theta{11}, …, \theta{1,p-1})
    \end{array}
    $$
    to obtain the first iterate.
  3. Repeat, as necessary.

An example

Consider the latent variable formulation of the binary probit model:
$$\begin{array}
{ll}
& z{i} = x{i}’ \beta + \varepsilon{i}, \ \varepsilon{i} \sim N(0,1) \
& y{i} = 0 \text{ if } z{i} < 0;\ y = 1 \text{ otherwise}.
\end{array}
$$

Random-Walk Metropolis algorithm

Algorithm

We can present the Gaussian Random-Walk Metropolis algorithm as follows:

  1. Start with $$ \theta_{0} $$ .
  2. Draw $$ \vartheta = \theta_{i} + \varepsilon $$ , $$ \varepsilon \sim N(0, s^{2} \Sigma) $$ .
  3. Compute $$ \alpha = min { 1, \pi(\vartheta) / \pi(\theta_{i}) } $$ .
  4. With probability $$ \alpha $$ to move and probability (1 - $$ \alpha $$ ) to stay.
  5. Repeat, as necessary.

In the RW Metropolis, we must choose the increment covariance matrix $$ \Sigma $$ and the scaling factor $$ s $$ . We often simply choose $$ \Sigma $$ to be either $$ I $$ or the asymptotic covariance matrix. Thereby, the scaling of the RW Metropolis is critical to its successful use. In practice, we can choose the initial scaling factor as a function of the dimension $$ s_{0} = 2.93 / \sqrt{d} $$ according to the Roberts and Rosenthal guidelines. For more details, please refer to the Chapter 3 in Rossi et al. (2012)[^rossi].
